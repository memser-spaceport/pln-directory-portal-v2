## Why PR Matters in Web3

In traditional tech, PR is mostly about customers and investors. In web3, it's also about developers, protocol communities, and a broader ecosystem that decides which projects are worth building on. A single CoinDesk article can do more for developer adoption than months of conference talks.

At the same time, web3 media has unique dynamics: crypto Twitter moves fast, narratives flip overnight, and journalists are skeptical of hype. Getting this right requires a different approach than standard startup PR.

## Before You Pitch: Get Your Story Straight

### The One-Liner
You need a crisp, jargon-free sentence that explains what you do. Test it on someone outside web3. If they're confused, you're not ready.

❌ "We're building a decentralized, permissionless, composable layer for trustless data sovereignty."
✅ "We help developers store files permanently without relying on AWS."

### The Why Now
Reporters want to know why this story matters today. Is there a regulatory shift, a technical unlock, or a market event that makes your timing relevant?

### The Proof Point
What have you built? Who's using it? Even small early metrics beat zero metrics. A protocol with 50 active developers is more newsworthy than a whitepaper promising 5,000.

## Target Media by Stage

### Early Stage (Pre-product / MVP)
- **Developer blogs and mirror.xyz** — write directly; no journalist needed
- **Protocol-specific communities** (Filecoin Slack, IPFS forums, ETH research)
- **Crypto Twitter / Farcaster** — build in public; share progress weekly

### Seed / Series A
- **The Block, Decrypt, CoinDesk** — good for fundraise announcements and product launches
- **TechCrunch / The Information** — harder to land, but valuable for mainstream credibility
- **Vertical tech press** — if you're in AI + web3, VentureBeat or Wired may be a better fit

### Growth Stage
- **WSJ, FT, Bloomberg** — reserved for significant milestones (large raises, major partnerships, regulatory moments)

## How to Pitch a Journalist

### Do Your Research First
Read their last 10 articles. Does your story fit what they actually cover? A mismatched pitch gets ignored and damages your relationship with that reporter.

### The Email Structure
- **Subject line:** "[Company] raises $X / launches Y / [specific hook]" — no fluff
- **First line:** The news in one sentence
- **Second paragraph:** Why it matters + one data point
- **Third paragraph:** Who you are, brief company background
- **Offer:** Are you pitching an exclusive? A briefing?

### Embargoes
For significant announcements, offer a 1–2 day embargo so the journalist can write a thorough piece before the news goes live. Honor embargoes religiously — breaking them destroys trust permanently.

## Common PR Mistakes in Web3

1. **Over-hyping before you have a product.** The community will call it out, and you'll spend months recovering.
2. **Token launches with no substance.** Press can't ignore tokenomics that don't make sense.
3. **Ignoring developer communities in favor of mainstream press.** Your best early press is word-of-mouth from builders using your protocol.
4. **No spokesperson prep.** Founders who ramble or go off-message in interviews lose narrative control.
5. **Treating bad news as no news.** Proactive communication during crises (hacks, pivots, delays) protects long-term trust.

## Template: Fundraise Announcement Press Release

```
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE

[Company] Raises $[X]M to [One-Line Mission]

[City, Date] — [Company], a [one-liner], today announced it has raised $[X]M in [round type] funding led by [lead investor], with participation from [others].

[2–3 sentences on what the company does and why it matters now.]

"[Quote from founder — make it non-generic.]"

[1 sentence on what the funding will be used for.]

About [Company]: [2 sentences.]

Contact: [Name, email]
```
