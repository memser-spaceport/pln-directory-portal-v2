## Why Web3 Hiring Is Different

The best engineers in the web3 space aren't just motivated by salary — they're motivated by mission, ownership, and the chance to build things that matter. This creates both a challenge and an opportunity for founders: you're competing for talent not just with other startups, but with well-funded protocols offering large token grants.

Understanding this dynamic is the first step to building a great team.

## Where to Find Web3 Engineers

### Within the PL Network
Your most underutilized resource. The PL Network Directory lists thousands of builders across the ecosystem. Many are open to new projects.

- Filter by skills (Rust, Go, Solidity, libp2p, etc.)
- Check "Open to Work" status
- Use office hours to get warm introductions from trusted founders

### Protocol-Specific Communities
Engineers who are already active in Filecoin, IPFS, libp2p, or other PL protocols are pre-qualified — they understand the stack and the values.

- GitHub issues and PRs are your best talent pipeline
- Sponsor hackathons (ETHGlobal, Devcon, etc.)
- Engage in Discord and Telegram communities authentically

### Remote-First Talent Pools
Web3 is genuinely global. Some of the best engineers are in Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, and Latin America. Don't constrain your search by geography.

## Compensation Benchmarks (2026)

These are approximate ranges for early-stage web3 startups with $2–10M raised:

| Role | Salary Range | Equity | Token Grant |
|------|-------------|--------|-------------|
| Senior Engineer | $180–240k | 0.25–0.75% | 0.1–0.3% (if applicable) |
| Staff Engineer | $220–280k | 0.5–1.0% | 0.2–0.5% |
| Engineering Manager | $200–260k | 0.4–0.8% | 0.15–0.4% |
| Head of Engineering | $240–300k | 0.75–1.5% | 0.3–0.75% |

*These are rough benchmarks — actual comp depends heavily on stage, location, and candidate.*

## Equity & Token Structures

### Standard Equity
4-year vest, 1-year cliff remains the default. Don't get creative here unless you have a good reason.

### Token Grants
If your project has a token (or anticipates one), many engineers will negotiate for token exposure. Common approaches:
- **Separate token pool** alongside equity, with its own vesting schedule
- **Phantom tokens** — economic rights without governance complications
- **Token options** — right to purchase at a fixed price

Consult the PLCS Legal Team on structuring token grants before you commit to anything.

## Evaluating Technical Candidates

### Technical Screen
- Focus on systems thinking, not just syntax
- Ask about trade-offs they've made in production systems
- Distributed systems experience is a strong signal

### Web3-Specific Assessment
- Can they articulate the trust assumptions in a system they've built?
- Do they understand the UX challenges of decentralized apps?
- Have they shipped something that real users depend on?

### Culture Fit
Mission alignment is not optional in web3. Engineers who are cynical about decentralization will drag down morale and product quality. Look for genuine curiosity about the space.

## Retention: The Real Challenge

Hiring is the easy part. Keeping people is harder.

### What works:
- **Meaningful technical problems.** Web3 engineers leave when the work becomes boring.
- **Ownership and autonomy.** Micromanagement kills intrinsic motivation.
- **Community.** Engineers want to be part of something larger. Conferences, open source contributions, and protocol involvement matter.
- **Transparent communication.** Founders who share the business reality (good and bad) build deeper loyalty.

### What doesn't work:
- Competing on salary alone — you'll always lose to the next hot protocol
- Titles without substance
- "Move fast and break things" culture — web3 engineers care about correctness and security
